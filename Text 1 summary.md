Core Concept: Life Unfolds in 4 Predictable Phases
- Limbo Phase: Feeling lost, not knowing what to do or want
- Vision Phase: A clear image of the future forms; you act and build momentum
- Flow Phase: Deep engagement where you can't pull away from your goal
- Resistance Phase: Progress plateaus; the challenge is to push through rather than quit

---

Step 1: Collect Vision
- Your mind understands the world through stories—feeling lost means you lack a clear narrative
- Give yourself permission to let life get worse: Sit with the question "If I keep doing the same things, where will my life end up?"
- Strong goals emerge from avoiding negative outcomes you never want to relive
- You need a problem to solve and an "enemy" to attack—this gives your goal gravity and clarity

---

Step 2: Change Your Mind
- You act the way you do because you already have a goal (often unconscious)
- Most people feeling lost are unconsciously pursuing the goal of staying the same to avoid pain, fear, and embarrassment of change
- Immerse yourself in new information: books, people, podcasts, courses, travel
- When your mind wants to escape your current trajectory, true learning occurs

---

Step 3: Gamify Your Life
- Design the game:
  - Create a hierarchy of goals: end goal → 1-year → 1-month → 1-week → daily tasks
  - Establish rules/constraints: what you won't sacrifice (health, relationships, etc.)
  - Use quantifiable daily tasks (e.g., write 1,000 words, read 10 pages) as feedback loops
- Create a tutorial phase: Learn by doing, not just watching—start before you feel ready
- Stay at the edge of the unknown: 
  - Too easy = boredom; too hard = anxiety
  - Gradually increase challenge like adding 2.5 lbs in the gym—small, consistent increments

---

Key Takeaways
- You don't need absolute clarity to start—just a general direction toward a "good life"
- Goals are lenses for decision-making, not rigid endpoints—they evolve as you grow
- AI can accelerate progress by acting as a "creative sparring partner" to overcome blocks
- A 9-to-5 becomes a "psychological death sentence" when you stop learning and face new challenges
