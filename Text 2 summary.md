Core Philosophy: Engagement vs. Addiction
- Focus on engaging players through fun design, not addicting them through psychological manipulation
- Avoids discussion of Skinner boxes, daily rewards, resource decay, loss aversion, and other exploitative mechanics

---

1. Pacing
- Gameplay pillars: Alternate between different activity types (combat, climbing, puzzles, set-pieces, cutscenes) before any one becomes stale
- Intensity modulation: Balance high-intensity moments with calmer periods to prevent exhaustion or desensitization
- Example: Uncharted 2 masterfully flows between puzzle-solving, firefights, exploration, and cinematic sequences
- Open world consideration: Provide diverse activities so players can self-modulate when bored

---

2. Novelty, Mystery & Anticipation
- Constantly introduce new ideas: mechanics, areas, enemy types to maintain curiosity
- Example: Mario games keep players engaged by making each level unpredictable despite similar core platforming
- Foreshadowing: Tease upcoming content to create anticipation before the reveal
  - The Witness: Unsolvable early door sticks in your mind
  - Dark Souls: Sen's Fortress visible but unreachable builds curiosity
- Metroidvania technique: Show environmental barriers you can't bypass yet, making players anticipate future abilities

---

3. Long-Term Goals & Progression
- Player expression: Let players build/create something unique to them (Stardew Valley farm customization)
- Short-term milestones: Community center, buildings, house upgrades provide immediate objectives on the path to larger ambitions
- Strategic planning: Long-term decisions (crops, seasons, relationships) keep players mentally engaged
- Exponential growth loops: Earn → invest → earn more (Stardew Valley, Monster Hunter)
- Automation/optimization: Progress from manual labor to self-running systems (Factorio, sprinklers in Stardew Valley)
- Fantasy fulfillment: Skill trees let players imagine future power and mastery

---

4. Compelling Challenge
- Flow state: Difficulty must be neither boring (too easy) nor stressful (too hard)
- Dynamic difficulty: Resident Evil 4 adjusts to keep players in the optimal zone
- Failure design: Short runs, visible improvement, and procedural variety (Tetris, Spelunky) encourage persistence despite frequent failure
- Diverse challenge types: Balance physical/reflex challenges with problem-solving, spatial awareness, and decision-making

---

Why Some Games Fail to Engage
- Doom: Excellent combat but exhausting pacing—long stretches of max intensity without modulation; lacks novelty compared to games like Titanfall 2

---

Key Insight
- Different engagement factors work for different players—identify what personally draws you in (novelty, mystery, challenge, progression, competition, social rewards, etc.)
